From Diffusion to Real-Time World Models

How video generation became interactive: a math-forward tour from flow matching and bidirectional diffusion, through the autoregressive “forcing” lineage that made long rollouts practical, to the memory and serving stack that shipped in 2026.

Audience · researchers & practitioners Reading time · ~35 min Figures · 10 interactive Last updated · June 2026

Eighteen months ago, “text-to-video” meant waiting a minute for a model to denoise a fixed 5-second clip. Today the same family of models runs as an interactive world simulator: you press a key, and the next frame — conditioned on everything you’ve done so far — arrives in 25 milliseconds. This post is the throughline of how that happened, and where the open problems moved.

Before you start — what we assume

This is written at researcher level. We assume you are comfortable with:

  • Diffusion models — the forward noising process, denoising/score objectives, DDPM/DDIM sampling. We give a compressed, rigorous recap, not a from-scratch tutorial.
  • Flow matching — learning a velocity field that transports noise to data along a probability path. Also recapped, not taught.
  • Transformers — self-attention, causal masking, and especially the KV cache as the mechanism behind autoregressive decoding.
  • Light familiarity with ODEs/SDEs, and the basic shape of imitation learning (helpful for the exposure-bias section).

If diffusion or flow matching are new to you, read Ho et al.1 and Lipman et al.4 first; everything downstream builds on them. Concepts we deliberately don’t derive (latent video VAEs, consistency models, RoPE, classifier-free guidance, the Dreamer line) are collected in Out of scope with pointers.

How to read the figures

Every figure is live. Drag sliders, toggle modes, press play. Animations pause when off-screen and respect prefers-reduced-motion. Figures marked schematic illustrate a mechanism — they are not running a real model. Numbers quoted from very recent or closed systems are tagged as reported in the references.

0The problem: from clips to worlds

Strip away the architectures and there is one task underneath all of this: model a distribution over video, p(x₁, x₂, …, x_T), and sample from it. What changed is the setting in which we sample.

An offline generator draws a whole clip at once. It can use the future to explain the past, denoise everything jointly, and take as long as it likes. An interactive world model cannot. It must emit frames in temporal order, react to inputs that arrive mid-generation, run at a fixed wall-clock cadence, and remember where it has been. The same probability model, served under a real-time causal constraint, becomes a different engineering object.

That shift surfaces three diseases. Almost every paper below is an answer to one of them:

  • Drift (exposure bias) — the modeling disease. An autoregressive model trained on ground-truth history must, at inference, condition on its own imperfect outputs. Errors compound until the video collapses.
  • Latency / streaming — the throughput disease. Joint bidirectional denoising can’t emit a frame until the whole clip is done, and costs O(T²) in clip length. Real-time interaction needs causal, few-step, streaming generation.
  • Memory — the consistency disease. Look away and look back: the world should still be there. Holding a coherent scene across minutes is the frontier problem of 2026.

Part 1 traces how drift and latency were beaten. Part 2 covers the 2025–2026 consolidation, the design-space debate it triggered, and the rise of memory. Part 3 is about what happens after the research succeeds: serving, and a library called FlashDreams.

Part 1 · From diffusion to Self Forcing++

1.1Diffusion & flow matching, fast

The problem this addresses: how do you learn to sample from a high-dimensional data distribution you only have samples from? Both diffusion and flow matching answer the same way — build a smooth bridge from an easy distribution (Gaussian noise) to the data, and learn to traverse it — but they parameterize the bridge differently.

Diffusion: corrupt, then learn to reverse

Fix a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to a clean sample x₀ over noise levels k = 0 … K. For the variance-preserving (DDPM) schedule it has the convenient closed form1

$$ x_k \;=\; \sqrt{\bar\alpha_k}\,x_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_k}\,\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I), $$

where ᾱ_k = ∏(1−β_i) decreases monotonically from 1 (clean) to 0 (pure noise). A single network is trained to undo one step of this corruption at every level, via the denoising / noise-prediction loss

$$ \mathcal L_{\text{diff}} \;=\; \mathbb E_{x_0,\,\epsilon,\,k}\,\big\lVert\, \epsilon_\theta(x_k, k) - \epsilon \,\big\rVert_2^2 . $$

Predicting the noise ε is equivalent (up to scaling) to learning the score ∇ₓ log p_k(x) ≈ −ε_θ / √(1−ᾱ_k).2

To sample, start from x_K ∼ 𝒩(0,I) and iterate the learned reverse step downward. Vanilla DDPM uses ~50–1000 steps; deterministic samplers like DDIM3 cut that substantially. Slide the level below to watch the bridge in both directions.

Flow matching: learn a velocity, integrate an ODE

Flow matching4 reframes the bridge as a deterministic transport. Define a time-indexed probability path p_t with p₀ = 𝒩(0,I) and p₁ = p_data, generated by a velocity field u_t(x) through the continuity equation. The simplest, and now dominant, choice is the linear (rectified) interpolant5: pair a noise sample x₀ with a data sample x₁ and connect them with a straight line,

$$ x_t \;=\; (1-t)\,x_0 + t\,x_1, \qquad t\in[0,1], \qquad u_t \;=\; \frac{dx_t}{dt} \;=\; x_1 - x_0. $$

The target velocity is just the constant displacement x₁−x₀. We regress a network onto it with the (conditional) flow-matching objective

$$ \mathcal L_{\text{CFM}} \;=\; \mathbb E_{t,\,x_0,\,x_1}\,\big\lVert\, v_\theta(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0) \,\big\rVert_2^2 , $$

and we sample by integrating the learned ODE from noise to data:

$$ \frac{dx}{dt} = v_\theta(x, t), \qquad x(0)\sim\mathcal N(0,I), \quad t:0\rightarrow 1. $$

Because the target paths are (marginally) straight, the ODE is nearly linear and a handful of Euler steps lands close to the data manifold — this is exactly the property that few-step samplers exploit later. The figure below transports a Gaussian blob onto a target shape; turn the step count down to feel how forgiving straight-line transport is.

Why the field standardized on flow matching. Same family of ideas as diffusion, but a cleaner continuous-time objective: no noise-schedule bookkeeping, stabler training, and straight paths that sample efficiently. Modern video models — Wan,7 CogVideoX,8 Cosmos9 — are overwhelmingly flow-matching DiTs6 operating in the latent space of a causal video VAE, not on raw pixels. We treat the VAE as a black box here (see Out of scope).

1.2Bidirectional video diffusion and its two fatal limits

The problem this addresses: the first strong video models denoised an entire clip jointly with full bidirectional attention — every frame attends to every other frame at every denoising step. This maximizes quality (each frame is explained using global context) but has two properties that are fatal for interaction.

  1. You cannot stream. Nothing is viewable until the whole clip has finished all denoising steps. There is no “first frame” to show.
  2. You cannot react. Future frames are co-determined with past frames in the same joint solve, so there is no causal seam at which to inject an action mid-generation.

It also caps length: attention over T frame-tokens costs O(T²) compute and memory, so doubling the clip quadruples the bill. World modeling is inherently causal — the next frame depends on the past and your action, not on a future that hasn’t happened — which is why the field moved to autoregressive (AR) generation: emit frames (or short chunks) in temporal order, conditioning on history through a KV cache, exactly like an LLM decodes tokens.

1.3The core disease: exposure bias & drift

The problem this addresses: the naïve way to train an AR video model is teacher forcing — condition on the ground-truth past, predict the next frame. Formally you fit

$$ p_\theta\!\left(x_t \,\middle|\, x^{\text{GT}}_{<t}\right) \quad\text{at training,}\qquad\text{but at inference you must use}\qquad p_\theta\!\left(x_t \,\middle|\, \hat x_{<t}\right), $$

conditioning on the model’s own generations x̂. This train/inference mismatch is exposure bias. The dynamics are vicious: a small error in an early frame makes the next context slightly out-of-distribution; the model, never trained on such context, errs a little more; that worse context feeds forward. The error e_t = ‖x̂_t − x_t‖ compounds, and within seconds you get the classic failure signatures — oversaturation, color shift, motion freezing — then full collapse.

This is precisely the covariate shift that imitation learning studies; DAgger32 is the canonical fix (train on your own induced state distribution), and that idea echoes through several methods below. Every technique in the rest of Part 1 is a different answer to one question: how do we close the gap between the context the model trained on and the context it actually sees at inference?

1.4Diffusion Forcing: noise as an uncertainty knob

The problem this addresses: teacher forcing trains on perfectly clean history; free-running inference feeds on imperfect history. Diffusion Forcing10 closes the gap with one elegant move: give every frame its own independent noise level during training.

Instead of a single global level k, assign a vector (k₁, …, k_T) and train the model to denoise frame t at its level k_t given past frames at arbitrary levels k_{<t}:

$$ p_\theta\!\left(x^{(k_t)}_t \,\middle|\, x^{(k_{<t})}_{<t}\right). $$

This single objective unifies the two regimes we’ve seen as corners of one cube:

  • Set all k_t = k equal ⇒ you recover full-sequence diffusion.
  • Set the history clean (k_{<t}=0) and only the current frame noised, with a causal mask ⇒ you recover next-frame autoregression.

The payoff is that noise becomes a knob for uncertainty. At inference you can keep the self-generated history partially noised rather than pretending it is clean. Because the model was trained on noisy context, conditioning on imperfect generations is now in-distribution instead of catastrophic — the drift loop is damped. Diffusion Forcing was the first method to roll out stably well past its training horizon. It also comes with a theoretical guarantee: the objective optimizes a variational lower bound on the likelihood of all sub-sequences.10 What it did not yet deliver was the quality and speed of the big bidirectional models. 1.5CausVid → Self Forcing: the distillation turn

The problem this addresses: training a strong causal model from scratch is hard and slow. The alternative that unlocked real-time generation: take a powerful but slow bidirectional teacher and distill it into a causal, few-step student.

CausVid: distribution-matching distillation, made causal

CausVid13 adapts a pretrained bidirectional diffusion transformer into an autoregressive one that generates frames on the fly, and slashes latency with Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD).11 The student is a few-step generator G_θ (≈4 steps instead of ≈50). DMD trains it not to match the teacher pixel-by-pixel, but to match the teacher’s output distribution, by minimizing a reverse KL whose gradient is a difference of scores:

$$ \nabla_\theta\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(p_\theta \,\|\, p_{\text{teacher}}\big) \;=\; \mathbb E_{x\sim G_\theta}\Big[\big(\underbrace{s_{\text{fake}}(x)}_{\nabla\log p_\theta} - \underbrace{s_{\text{real}}(x)}_{\nabla\log p_{\text{teacher}}}\big)\,\nabla_\theta G_\theta\Big]. $$

s_real is the frozen teacher score; s_fake is an auxiliary score network tracking the student’s own (moving) distribution. Where they disagree, the gradient pushes the student toward the teacher’s manifold.

Where ODE distillation fits. Before DMD, many systems first teach the causal student to follow the teacher’s probability-flow ODE: the teacher traces a deterministic path from noise to clean latents, and the student is initialized to reproduce that path in far fewer steps. This is the “make the sampler fast and approximately correct” stage; distribution matching then cleans up the student’s output distribution, and self-rollout training addresses the history it will actually condition on.

Two tricks make it stable: a student initialization from the teacher’s ODE trajectories, and an asymmetric setup where a causal student is supervised by a bidirectional teacher. The result streamed high-quality video at 9.4 FPS on a single GPU with KV caching, topping VBench-Long.13 This is what made real-time frame rates plausible. But CausVid still trained on ground-truth context — exposure bias remained.

Self Forcing: train the way you infer

Self Forcing14 closed that last loop. During training, the student actually rolls out autoregressively, generating each frame conditioned on its own previously generated frames via the KV cache — exactly the inference procedure — and the distribution-matching loss is applied holistically over the whole rolled-out video rather than per frame:

$$ \mathcal L_{\text{SF}} \;=\; \mathcal D\Big(\;\hat x_{1:T}\sim G_\theta\big(\cdot\mid \hat x_{<t}\big)\;,\;\; p_{\text{teacher}}\Big), \qquad \mathcal D \in \{\text{DMD},\,\text{GAN}\}. $$

Because training simulates inference, the model learns to operate on its own outputs — the train/inference gap of §1.3 is closed by construction, not by a noise trick. Combined with few-step generation and a stochastic gradient-truncation trick for efficiency, Self Forcing delivered real-time, streaming, sub-second-latency video on a single GPU, while matching or beating much slower non-causal models. It became an important open-source reference point — usually built on Wan2.1 (1.3B / 14B)7 — for the forcing-family systems that followed.14

The recipe, so far. Bidirectional flow-matching teacher → causal few-step student via DMD → self-rollout training so the student trains on its own context. The full four-stage pipeline (with the memory stage still to come) is in Figure 7.

1.6Self Forcing++: supervising beyond the teacher’s horizon

The problem this addresses: Self Forcing is still bounded by the teacher’s horizon — about 5 seconds. Extrapolate further and drift returns, because nothing ever supervised the model at minute-scale positions.

Self Forcing++15 makes a sharp observation: you don’t need a long-horizon teacher to supervise long videos. Let the student roll out for minutes. Then sample short windows from deep inside that rollout, re-noise them, and apply DMD against the ordinary short-horizon teacher on those windows:

  1. Roll out a long trajectory x̂₁:ₙ with the student (N up to minutes).
  2. Sample a window W = x̂_{a:a+L} with a ≫ the teacher’s horizon, where drift would normally appear.
  3. Backward-noise it per the schedule, W_k = √(ᾱ_k)·W + √(1−ᾱ_k)·ε, and apply extended DMD on W_k.

The teacher only ever sees a clip of the length it was trained on, but the student receives corrective gradient at positions far beyond the training horizon. With a rolling KV cache and extended RoPE31 for the longer position range, this reached minute-scale generation — up to 4 min 15 s, ~50× the baseline and ~99.9% of the base model’s positional span — with no long ground-truth video and no recomputation of overlapping frames.15

By late 2025, the open literature had the two pieces the field had been missing: real-time causal few-step generation (Self Forcing) plus minute-scale stability (Self Forcing++). On the closed/world-model side, Genie 320 had already demonstrated interactive environments — 720p, 24 FPS, consistent for several minutes, with visual memory reaching ~1 minute back — but the system remained closed and architecturally unexplained.

Part 2 · Late 2025 → mid 2026

2.1The long-horizon “forcing” family consolidated

The problem this addresses: Self Forcing++ proved minute-scale was possible, but how exactly should you structure the rollout and its attention to keep quality high while staying real-time? Three concurrent works became the standard trio, all built on windowed distillation but differing in the details.

  • Self Forcing++15 — relies on the historical KV cache alone (described above).
  • Rolling Forcing16 (ICLR 2026) — jointly denoises multiple frames with progressively increasing noise levels across a rolling window, deliberately relaxing strict frame-by-frame causality so neighbouring frames refine each other and break the error-accumulation chain; and borrows the attention-sink mechanism from StreamingLLM30 to pin the KV states of the initial frames as a global anchor.
  • LongLive17 — uses frame-level sink frames plus KV re-caching to support smooth prompt switching mid-stream, with short-window attention; sustains 20.7 FPS on a single H100 and up to 240-second videos.

The throughline worth noticing: LLM systems ideas are migrating wholesale into video. Attention sinks, StreamingLLM-style fixed-window caches, KV re-caching — all imported from text decoding. Switch Figure 3 to Rolling window + sinks to see the banded local window with a few pinned sink columns; that single mask is the shared structure under this whole family, and it is why cost drops from O(T²) to O(T·w).

2.2People started questioning the recipe itself

The problem this addresses: the Self Forcing recipe asks one distribution-matching loss to do two hard jobs at once — close the few-step gap (4 steps vs 50) and close the conditioning gap (clean vs self-generated history). Is that a good idea?

Causal Forcing18 (thu-ml, ICML 2026) argues that the initialization step itself matters. If ODE distillation asks a causal student to imitate a bidirectional teacher, the teacher’s flow map can depend on future frames that the student will never see; the paper frames this as an architectural / injectivity gap that pushes the student toward a conditional-average solution. Their fix is to use an autoregressive teacher for ODE initialization before self-rollout training, so the few-step sampler and the causal conditioning structure are aligned before DMD-style refinement. They stress it is orthogonal to LongLive and Rolling Forcing (and later added Rolling-Forcing support for minute-level generation).

Separately, BAgger19 attacks drift without distillation at all: a DAgger-flavoured32 self-supervised scheme that builds corrective trajectories from the model’s own rollouts and trains with standard score / flow-matching objectives — avoiding large teachers and backprop through long chains, on the argument that distillation plus distribution-matching losses can hurt quality and diversity.

The takeaway: the field is no longer monolithically “DMD on self-rollouts.” There is a genuine design-space debate now — how many distillation stages, whether to use a teacher at all, how strictly to enforce causality.

2.3Memory became the headline problem

The problem this addresses: once real-time and minute-scale stability became practical in reported systems, the obvious remaining gap with Genie 3 was spatial consistency. Generate for a minute, pan the camera away, pan back — does the scene persist, or does the model invent a new one? This is the look-away/look-back test.

The 2026 development is open models seriously attacking this, mostly by making the model attend over a larger or smarter slice of history:

  • LingBot-World21 (Robbyant / Ant Group, Jan 2026, open weights, built on Wan2.2 as a 28B mixture-of-experts DiT). Minute-level horizons with contextual consistency (“long-term memory”), and a distilled Fast variant using block-causal attention + diffusion forcing + DMD for ~16 FPS at 480p on one GPU node with sub-second latency — an open Genie-3-alike, achieved largely by scaling the context the diffusion model attends over.
  • RELIC25 (Adobe, Dec 2025) — the most architecturally interesting on memory. It represents long-horizon memory as highly compressed historical latent tokens, encoded with relative actions and absolute camera poses, inside the KV cache. That makes the cache pose-aware: revisiting a viewpoint aligns to the stored tokens, giving implicit 3D-consistent retrieval cheaply — no explicit 3D reconstruction. A memory-efficient self-forcing variant lets it distill full-context from a teacher fine-tuned beyond its 5-s horizon. 14B, real-time at 16 FPS.
  • Matrix-Game 3.022 (Skywork, ~2026) — the synthesis. It frames the landscape crisply: earlier real-time models (Matrix-Game 2.0, Hunyuan-GameCraft24) had streaming interaction but lacked minute-long memory; LingBot-World improved geometric consistency by scaling context but struggled to hold memory and real-time deployment together. Matrix-Game 3.0 claims all three at once via a camera-aware memory mechanism and training–inference-aligned multi-segment distillation: 40 FPS at 720p with a 5B model, stable memory over minute-long sequences, plus a 28B scale-up for better dynamics.

2.4The application layer arrived

The problem this addresses: are these still demos, or are they being deployed into systems that act? In 2026, the latter.

  • Robotics — unified video-action world modeling: predict the next chunk of frames via conditional flow matching in a single causal AR process with persistent KV-cache memory and real-time observation integration; the same model that imagines the future also proposes the action.
  • RL environments — Dreamer-style27 model-based RL is the older world-model tradition; the new generative-video systems are beginning to fill the same role as interactive environments for long-horizon policy learning.
  • Autonomous vehicles — NVIDIA’s OmniDreams26 (GTC 2026) is the AV instantiation: a world model mid- and post-trained from Cosmos9 on 21k hours of driving data to autoregressively generate action-conditioned video in real time, replacing reconstruction-based neural simulators that can’t generalize beyond captured data. It runs closed-loop with the Alpamayo policy: the policy’s actions update simulator state, which shapes the next generated sensor observations. Tellingly, its training stack is exactly the lineage above — the repo ships configs for student-init, bidirectional teacher, and self-forcing distillation.

Part 3 · FlashDreams — the serving layer

3.1FlashDreams is “vLLM for world models”

Here is the punchline first: FlashDreams is not a new generation method.28 It is a high-performance inference and serving library for interactive autoregressive video and world models — a general runtime for real-time world-model applications across gaming, AVs, robotics, and virtual environments. It began as the runtime behind the OmniDreams closed-loop GTC 2026 demo and grew into a platform. The “vLLM29 for world models” analogy is the authors’ own.

The problem this addresses: the research of Parts 1–2 made the model fast enough in principle. But a live interactive session has different systems requirements than offline generation. The analogy is exact and worth stating precisely:

Two autoregressive loops

LLM chat: prefill → decode → decode → … → prefill → decode → … — turn-based. Each user turn re-prefills a growing prompt, then decodes a response.

Interactive world model: initialize → decode → decode → decode → … — initialize a session once, then advance the world continuously at a fixed pace, forever, folding in control inputs as they arrive.

Both are autoregressive, but the world-model loop is a steady-state stream, not a request/response. That difference is the entire reason a separate serving system is justified. Figure 8 contrasts the two timelines.

3.2The systems problem & its optimizations

A persistent low-latency session has requirements that one-shot generators (and offline-throughput libraries like FastVideo / LightX2V) simply don’t: low latency when controls change, a GPU kept busy across AR steps, frames streamed at a steady pace, and rolling world state carried forward across steps. FlashDreams’ optimizations, as described for the OmniDreams runtime, are classic AR-inference systems engineering applied to video:

  • Streaming static-shape KV cache. A pre-allocated, fixed-size cache (a ring buffer) instead of one that dynamically grows. Fixed shapes mean no reallocation, no fragmentation, and — crucially — a forward pass whose tensor shapes never change step to step.
  • Fixed-size local attention window. Attend over a bounded recent window instead of the full history, capping per-step cost at O(w) regardless of how long the session has run. (Memory mechanisms from §2.3 are what let you do this without forgetting the world.)
  • CUDA Graphs. Because the shapes are static, the entire forward pass can be captured once and replayed, eliminating per-step Python / framework launch overhead — which dominates when each step is only milliseconds.

On top of these it adds a production serving backend: persistent low-latency sessions, multi-GPU execution, and streaming I/O. Figure 9 shows why static shapes + CUDA graphs matter so much when the per-step compute is tiny: framework overhead that is negligible offline becomes the bottleneck at 25 ms/frame.

3.3Real numbers: where the milliseconds go

Interactive video lives and dies by a single arithmetic fact:

$$ \Delta t_{\text{frame}} \;=\; \frac{1000\ \text{ms}}{\text{FPS}}, \qquad \text{and per frame}\quad \Delta t_{\text{frame}} \;\approx\; N_{\text{steps}}\times \ell_{\text{step}}. $$

The frame budget is brutal and fixed: 40 FPS = 25 ms/frame, 24 FPS = 41.7 ms, 20.7 FPS = 48 ms, 16 FPS = 62.5 ms. Everything — the denoising steps, the attention, the VAE decode, the framework overhead — has to fit inside it. The second equation is why few-step generation is non-negotiable: at N=50 steps you’d need each step under 0.5 ms to hit 40 FPS, which is hopeless; at N=4 you have ~6 ms/step to work with. Distillation buys the steps; the runtime buys back the overhead.

FlashDreams reports end-to-end speedups of 2.12× on Self-Forcing, 3.10× on LingBot-World, and 1.40× on Wan2.128 — pure serving wins, with no change to the model weights. The interactive below lets you set a target frame rate, pick a model, and apply the FlashDreams speedup to see whether the frame budget is met.

3.4The model zoo is the research arc

The clearest evidence that FlashDreams is the packaging of everything above: its model zoo reads like the bibliography of this post. It ships runnable integrations for Self-Forcing, OmniDreams, LingBot-World, Causal-Forcing, Causal Wan2.2, FlashVSR, Wan2.1, and Cosmos-Predict2.5 — literally the lineage from Parts 1–2, as Apache-2.0 software with a PyPI package. The quickstart is a Self-Forcing Wan2.1-T2V-1.3B run. Even bidirectional models fold in, by treating bidirectional inference as a single-rollout autoregressive pass.28

The meta-story

Where the frontier went

The real answer to “how far has the field come?” is a shift in what kind of problem is open. When this lineage began, the open problems were algorithmic: how do you train a causal model that doesn’t drift? Those are now mitigated well enough in reported systems — via the Self Forcing lineage and its competitors — that the frontier has bifurcated:

  • (a) Memory / consistency as the remaining modeling problem — holding a coherent, navigable world across minutes (RELIC, Matrix-Game 3.0, LingBot-World).
  • (b) Serving infrastructure as the new systems problem — persistent, low-latency, multi-GPU streaming sessions (FlashDreams).

FlashDreams existing at all — with vLLM-style positioning, an Apache-2.0 license, a PyPI package, and a Discord — is the clearest signal that interactive world models are crossing from papers into deployable software. Exposure bias, real-time, and memory were three separate research questions; the emerging stack treats them as stages of one training run, served by one runtime.

What is still not solved

The hard edges moved rather than disappeared. Reported FPS numbers are not controlled benchmarks; closed systems expose capability but not mechanism; generated worlds can still hallucinate or lose control fidelity; memory can fail under large viewpoint changes, dynamic objects, or long interaction loops; and the field still lacks a shared evaluation suite for interactive consistency, action responsiveness, and policy usefulness.

A re-entry reading list

  1. Causal Forcing18 — the sharpest critique of the standard recipe.
  2. RELIC25 or Matrix-Game 3.022 — the memory frontier.
  3. OmniDreams26 — the full-stack deployed application.
  4. Then clone FlashDreams28 — it runs the models from all of the above on your own hardware.

Out of scope (but worth reading)

To keep the throughline tight, several load-bearing concepts were treated as black boxes. If you want to go deeper, these are the gaps and where to start:

  • Latent video tokenization / causal VAEs — everything here happens in a compressed latent space, not pixels. The VAE’s temporal compression sets the real frame budget.
  • Score matching & the SDE/ODE formulation — the rigorous bridge between diffusion and flow (Song et al.2).
  • Fast samplers & consistency models — DDIM3 and consistency/ODE-distillation underpin the “few-step” claims; Causal Forcing leans on them directly.
  • Classifier-free guidance — how text/action conditioning strength is controlled at sample time.
  • GAN / adversarial distillation — the alternative to DMD as the holistic loss in self-rollout training.
  • RoPE & positional extrapolation31, StreamingLLM / attention sinks30 — the LLM machinery being imported into video.
  • Imitation learning / DAgger32 — the formal lens on exposure bias; BAgger is essentially DAgger for video.
  • RL world models (the Dreamer line)27 — the other tradition of “world model,” now converging with generative video.
  • 3D reconstruction (NeRF, Gaussian splatting) — the explicit-geometry approach that pose-aware KV caches deliberately avoid.
  • PagedAttention / vLLM internals29, quantization, speculative decoding — the serving-systems toolbox FlashDreams adapts from LLM-land.
